What is a Registered Ecological Garden?

A Registered Ecological Garden is a private residential property where the garden is actively managed for ecological improvement and recorded in a central registry. The property owner commits to measurable ecological practices—such as native plantings, habitat creation, soil restoration, and long-term stewardship—and these actions are documented, monitored, and aggregated over time. The registry serves as a permanent system of record, making private ecological work countable and allowing councils, planners, and researchers to understand the cumulative environmental value of residential land.

Why this exists

The problem

Across cities, significant ecological work occurs in private gardens—native plantings, carbon sequestration, habitat restoration, soil regeneration. Yet this work remains invisible to local government and urban planning systems. Councils cannot measure it, planners cannot incorporate it, and researchers cannot study it at scale. The result: private land doing ecological work goes uncounted in environmental policy, biodiversity targets, and climate planning.

The opportunity

If we treat private gardens as ecological infrastructure—measured, recorded, and aggregated—we create a new category of urban environmental asset. With standardised measurement and centralised registration, thousands of individual residential properties can be understood collectively. This creates visibility for home stewards, enables councils to recognise ecological contributions in policy, and builds a long-term dataset for institutions tracking urban biodiversity and carbon outcomes.

How the Registry works

The Registry operates as a simple, repeatable process for documenting and aggregating ecological work in residential gardens.

  1. Garden stewardship & ecological planting
    Property owners manage their gardens for ecological outcomes: native species, habitat structure, soil health, water management.
  2. Measurement
    Gardens are assessed using standardised metrics: plant species and quantities, canopy structure, habitat features, soil carbon, water retention capacity.
  3. Registration in a central registry
    Property details and ecological measurements are recorded in the Registry. Each garden receives a unique identifier. Participation is voluntary; data is held securely.
  4. Aggregated, anonymised outcomes
    The Registry produces aggregated reports for councils, planners, and researchers—total native plantings, carbon sequestration, biodiversity value—without exposing individual property information.

Who it's for

  • Home gardeners Property owners committed to ecological gardening. The Registry recognises and records your stewardship, making long-term ecological work visible and measurable.
  • Councils & urban planners Local government seeking to understand and incorporate private land contributions to urban biodiversity, climate targets, and green infrastructure planning.
  • Researchers & institutions Ecological researchers, universities, and environmental organisations studying urban biodiversity, residential carbon sequestration, and the role of private land in environmental outcomes.
